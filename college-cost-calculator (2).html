What will college really cost your family?

Award letters can be confusing on purpose. This worksheet walks through the same math a college counselor would do with you: add up the full cost, subtract the aid, and find the number that matters — what your family is actually being asked to pay.

1 · Add up the real costTuition and housing, but also books, travel, and shampoo. Colleges publish these numbers — use theirs.
2 · Subtract free moneyGrants and scholarships never have to be paid back. This is the best kind of aid.
3 · Subtract loans — carefullyLoans lower today's bill, but they're borrowed. You will pay them back with interest.
4 · Find the bottom lineWhat's left is what your family must cover — and how it compares to what FAFSA says you can afford.

Compare your schools

The sticker price is not the real price. A school that looks twice as expensive can end up costing less once aid is counted — always compare bottom lines, not tuition.

What do these words mean?

Cost of Attendance (COA)

The college's estimate of one full year: tuition, fees, housing, food, books, travel, and personal expenses. It's the starting number all aid is measured against.

Direct vs. indirect costs

Direct costs are billed by the college (tuition, dorm, meal plan). Indirect costs are real expenses the college never bills you for — books, bus or plane tickets home, laundry, toothpaste. Budget roughly $4,000+ per year for indirect costs, depending on the student.

Pell Grant

Money from the federal government for students with financial need, based on your FAFSA. It's free money — you never pay it back. The maximum changes each year (it has been around $7,400).

SEOG Grant

The Supplemental Educational Opportunity Grant — additional federal free money for students with the highest need, awarded by the college from a limited pool. Also never repaid.

Grants & scholarships from the school

Free money awarded by the college itself, from need-based grants to merit scholarships. At expensive private colleges this can be the biggest single line in your package — which is why sticker price alone tells you almost nothing.

Subsidized vs. unsubsidized loans

Both are federal student loans that must be repaid. On a subsidized loan, the government pays the interest while you're in school. On an unsubsidized loan, interest starts adding up from day one. If you borrow, subsidized first.

Work-study

A campus job set aside for students with financial need. Important: it's money you earn during the year, paycheck by paycheck — it won't reduce the bill the college sends in August, and you must actually find and keep the job.

SAI (Student Aid Index, formerly EFC)

The number the FAFSA calculates as what your family can reasonably contribute for one year. Colleges use it to decide aid — but many don't fully meet your need, which creates the gap below.

The "gap"

The difference between what the college is asking your family to pay and what the FAFSA says your family can afford. A large gap means you'd need outside scholarships, extra work, or private loans to attend — and it's a fair reason to appeal your aid or choose a different school.

All numbers are per year. Use the figures from each college's official cost-of-attendance page and your own financial aid award letters. This worksheet is for planning and comparison — your financial aid office has the final word.
